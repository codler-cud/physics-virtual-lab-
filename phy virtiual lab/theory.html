🎯   Aim of the Experiment ▼

Objective: To determine the line density (number of lines per unit length, denoted by ρ) of a given diffraction grating using a LASER source of known wavelength (λ = 635 nm).

The line density is a fundamental property of any diffraction grating and is critical in spectroscopy, telecommunications, and optical engineering. By measuring how the laser light diffracts through the grating, we can calculate this value with high precision.

📖   Theory & Principle ▼

Diffraction is the bending of light waves around obstacles or through narrow openings. A diffraction grating is an optical element with a large number of equally spaced parallel slits.

When monochromatic light (such as from a LASER) passes through a grating, it produces a pattern of bright spots called maxima on a distant screen. The central bright spot is the zeroth order maximum, and the spots on either side are the first order (n=1) maxima.

d · sin(θ) = n · λ

Where: d = grating element (distance between adjacent slits), θ = angle of diffraction, n = order of diffraction, λ = wavelength of light.

Since the spot distance x from center and screen distance D are measurable:

tan(θ) = x / D   →   θ = tan⁻¹(x / D)

The line density ρ is the reciprocal of d:

ρ = 1/d = sin(θ) / (n · λ)
🔧   Apparatus Required ▼
  • LASER Source — He-Ne or Diode Laser (λ = 635 nm)
  • Diffraction Grating — Standard transmission grating
  • Optical Bench — A stable, aligned rail for mounting components
  • White Screen — For observing the diffraction pattern
  • Measuring Scale — Meter ruler for measuring D and x
  • Grating Holder & Stands — For precise alignment
📋   Step-by-Step Procedure ▼
  1. Mount the laser source at one end of the optical bench and the diffraction grating in its holder perpendicular to the laser beam.
  2. Place the white screen at a measured distance D from the grating (e.g., 15 cm).
  3. Switch on the laser. Align it so the beam passes straight through the grating centre.
  4. Observe the diffraction pattern on the screen — a bright central spot and first-order spots on either side.
  5. Measure the distance of the left spot (x₁) and right spot (x₂) from the central maximum using the scale.
  6. Move the screen to a new distance D and repeat the measurement.
  7. Record at least 3 observations at different values of D.
